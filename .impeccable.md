# Service Compris — Design Context

## Design Context

### Users
Casual browser gamers looking for surprise and fun. Not hardcore sim players. They stumble in, get hooked by the absurdity and tension, and keep coming back to prove they can survive a shift. They're not reading the manual — they're learning by doing and getting caught off guard.

### Brand Personality
**Frantic. Suspicious. Arrogant.**
The interface itself has an attitude. It's not friendly software — it's a demanding Parisian restaurant that doesn't apologize for being difficult. It treats the player with mild contempt, like a new hire who probably won't last the night.

Adding: **Surprising.** The game should catch players off guard — in the UI, in the interactions, in small moments of unexpected personality.

### Emotional Goal
Survival. Not success — *survival*. At the end of a shift, the player should feel: *"I made it through that chaos."* The relief of clocking out. Exhaustion mixed with pride. The design should make a shift feel genuinely demanding so the ending feels earned.

### Reference: Hades
- Dramatic, stylized visuals with strong personality
- Dark palette with bursts of saturated color for key moments
- **Sound design is integral to the experience** — every interaction has a voice
- The UI has character; menus feel like they belong to the world
- Bold, expressive typography that feels like the world is talking to you

### Reference: Papers Please
- Bureaucratic tension — mundane documents carry moral weight
- Oppressive atmosphere; the UI itself creates pressure
- Monochrome-ish base with color used only for alerts and decisions
- The act of checking documents feels consequential
- Rhythm: repetitive mechanics become ritual, then suddenly break

### Anti-References
- City simulators: no clean, optimistic, "growing city" energy
- Real simulators: no hyper-realistic corporate UI, no tutorial-heavy UX, no progress bars for the sake of it
- No cozy game aesthetics — warm doesn't mean safe here

### Aesthetic Direction
**Night-shift noir meets arrogant French bureaucracy.**

The current cream/gold palette is charming but too cozy. The shift should feel like it happens at night, in a restaurant that's slightly too full, slightly too loud, and definitely judging you. Push toward:

- **Darker base**: Deep charcoal or near-black backgrounds for in-game UI. The cream stays for paper/document elements (ledger, tickets, corkboard) to make them feel like physical objects under a lamp.
- **Gold as earned**: The warm gold/brass stays but is used sparingly — it should feel like something achieved, not ambient decoration.
- **Warm but tense**: The warmth comes from candlelight and brass, not from a pastel background. Darkness makes the warm accents pop.
- **Chunky drop shadows stay**: They ground elements physically, like objects on a desk.

Theme: **Dark with warm pockets of light.** Think: documents under a desk lamp in a dark room.

### Sound as Design Material
Sound is called out as important. Design implications:
- Every interactive element should feel like it has a click, a stamp, a shuffle
- Transitions should feel audibly present (even if subtle)
- Error states and successes should be distinguishable by feel, not just color
- Silence is also a design choice — use it before big reveals

### Design Principles

1. **Earned relief over comfort** — The UI should resist the player slightly. A shift that felt hard makes the end screen feel like survival. Don't make it easy to look at; make it satisfying to finish.

2. **Bureaucratic drama** — The mundane (ledger, queue, timestamps) should feel *consequential*, not administrative. A guest's name in the ledger is a verdict. A time stamp is evidence. Treat data like it matters.

3. **Arrogant personality** — The interface has an attitude. Labels, UI chrome, empty states — all should carry the restaurant's voice: demanding, slightly condescending, efficient. Not warm-friendly, not cold-corporate.

4. **Sound-native interactions** — Design with the assumption that every tap/click/stamp has audio. Visual feedback should *amplify* sound, not replace it. Short, punchy interactions over long transitions.

5. **Surprising details** — At least one unexpected moment per feature. Something that makes a player say "I didn't know it did that." The interface should have secrets.

### Color Direction
- **Base**: Deep charcoal/near-black (not pure `#000` — tint warm toward the gold hue)
- **Surface**: Dark warm panels — think aged wood, dark brass, dim light
- **Paper elements**: Cream (`#E4E3E0` family) — documents, ledger, tickets — feel like physical objects
- **Accent**: Warm gold/brass (`#c8a84b`, `#f0c040`) — used sparingly, earned
- **Alert states**: Keep the red/amber system but let them *cut through* the dark base
- **VIP/success**: Deep emerald, not bright green

### Typography Direction
- Display/headers: Something with personality — not a default sans. Needs to feel like it was typeset, not generated. Condensed, confident, slightly aggressive.
- Body/UI labels: Tight, utilitarian, uppercase. The current all-caps pattern is correct.
- Document elements (ledger, tickets): A different register — typewriter or serif. Physical.
- Monospace for data (time, cash, morale): Correct — keep it.
